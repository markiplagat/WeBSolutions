Peak Pressure Index (PPI)
{{ frame.peak_pressure_index|floatformat:0|default:"—" }} out of 4,095
{% if frame.peak_pressure_index > 3000 %}High {% elif frame.peak_pressure_index > 2000 %}Moderate {% else %}Good{% endif %}
Alert threshold
0 — No pressure 4,095 — Maximum
What it is: The highest pressure recorded at any single point on the mat during this reading, but only in areas where at least 10 sensor cells are active (to filter out noise). Think of it like the hottest spot on a thermal camera image of your seat.
What it means for you: {% if frame.peak_pressure_index > 3000 %} Your reading of {{ frame.peak_pressure_index|floatformat:0 }} is in the high risk zone. One area of your body is bearing too much weight on the mat. This kind of sustained pressure can reduce blood flow to the skin, which over time can cause a pressure ulcer (also called a bedsore). You should reposition yourself now. {% elif frame.peak_pressure_index > 2000 %} Your reading of {{ frame.peak_pressure_index|floatformat:0 }} is moderately elevated. Pressure is building in one spot. You do not need to act immediately, but try to shift your weight slightly within the next few minutes. {% else %} Your reading of {{ frame.peak_pressure_index|floatformat:0 }} is in the normal range. Your weight is well distributed and there are no areas of concern right now. Keep up your regular repositioning routine. {% endif %}
What should I do?
{% if frame.peak_pressure_index > 3000 %}
  • Lean to your left or right side for 30–60 seconds to shift the pressure
  • Tilt your chair back slightly if possible
  • Try a forward lean — place your hands on your knees and lean forward
  • If the alert keeps appearing, contact your clinician
{% elif frame.peak_pressure_index > 2000 %}
  • Do a small weight shift — lean slightly to one side
  • Make sure you are sitting centrally on the mat
  • Plan your next full repositioning within the next 15–20 minutes
{% else %}
  • Continue your regular repositioning every 15–30 minutes
  • Keep monitoring — pressure can build gradually
{% endif %}

Seat Contact Area
{{ frame.contact_area_percent|floatformat:1|default:"—" }} % of mat covered
{% if frame.contact_area_percent < 20 %}Low {% elif frame.contact_area_percent > 75 %}Wide {% else %}Normal{% endif %}
0% — Not seated 50% — Half mat 100% — Full mat
What it is: The percentage of the sensor mat that your body is actually touching. Imagine the mat as a grid of 1,024 tiny squares — this number tells you how many of those squares have any weight on them. A higher percentage generally means your weight is spread more evenly.
What it means for you: {% if frame.contact_area_percent < 20 %} Only {{ frame.contact_area_percent|floatformat:1 }}% of the mat is in contact. This is unusually low, which often means your weight is concentrated in a very small area — like just your tailbone or one side. This can be uncomfortable and increases pressure ulcer risk even if the PPI looks acceptable. {% elif frame.contact_area_percent > 75 %} {{ frame.contact_area_percent|floatformat:1 }}% of the mat is in contact. This is a wide spread, meaning your weight is distributed across a large area. This is generally positive, though very high values can sometimes indicate slouching or an unusual posture — worth checking with your clinician. {% else %} {{ frame.contact_area_percent|floatformat:1 }}% of the mat is in contact. This is a normal, healthy range. Your weight is distributed over a good portion of the seat surface, which helps prevent pressure concentrating in one spot. {% endif %}
What should I do?
{% if frame.contact_area_percent < 20 %}
  • Check your sitting posture — are you perched on one side?
  • Try to sit back into the seat so more of your thighs are on the mat
  • Mention this pattern to your clinician at your next review
{% elif frame.contact_area_percent > 75 %}
  • Check you are sitting upright and not slouching
  • Ask your clinician to review your seating position at your next appointment
{% else %}
  • Your seating position looks good — keep it up
  • Continue repositioning regularly as advised by your clinician
{% endif %}
